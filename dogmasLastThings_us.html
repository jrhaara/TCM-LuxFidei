Dogmas about the Last Things

Dogmas about the last things, also known as eschatology, reveal truths about the end of times, death, particular and final judgment, heaven, purgatory, and hell.

Death and Judgment

The dogma of death and judgment proclaims that each person, upon dying, receives an immediate particular judgment, in which God determines their eternal destiny based on their actions, faith, and state of grace. This particular judgment precedes the universal final judgment that will occur at the end of time.

Biblical basis: Luke 16:22-23, Hebrews 9:27, 2 Corinthians 5:10

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, confirmed by the Councils

Historical context: The doctrine of particular judgment was always part of Christian faith, establishing that death is not the end, but the beginning of eternal life, whether in heaven, purgatory, or hell.

Heaven

The dogma of heaven proclaims that heaven is the state of eternal and perfect happiness in the presence of God. Those who die in a state of grace and without mortal sin enter immediately into heaven, where they enjoy the beatific vision, seeing God face to face and participating in his divine life.

Biblical basis: Matthew 25:34, Revelation 21:1-4, 1 Corinthians 2:9

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, confirmed by the Council of Florence (1439)

Historical context: Heaven was always understood as the final destination of the just. The Council of Florence defined that the souls of the saints enter immediately into heaven after death, enjoying the beatific vision.

Purgatory

The dogma of purgatory proclaims that there exists a state of purification after death for those who die in a state of grace, but with unforgiven venial sins or with temporal debt of already forgiven sins. Purgatory is temporary and ends when the soul is completely purified.

Biblical basis: 2 Maccabees 12:46, 1 Corinthians 3:15, Matthew 12:32

Proclaimed: Council of Florence (1439), Council of Trent (1563)

Historical context: In response to Protestantism that denied purgatory, the Council of Trent reaffirmed this doctrine, establishing that souls in purgatory can be helped by the prayers and suffrages of the faithful.

Hell

The dogma of hell proclaims that hell is a state of eternal suffering for those who die in mortal sin without repentance. Hell consists in the eternal privation of the vision of God (penalty of loss) and in sufferings proportional to the sins committed (penalty of sense).

Biblical basis: Matthew 25:41, Mark 9:43, Revelation 20:15

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, confirmed by the Council of Florence (1439)

Historical context: The existence of hell was always part of Christian faith, establishing that the definitive rejection of God leads to eternal damnation. It was reaffirmed against theories that denied the eternity of hell.

The Second Coming

The dogma of the second coming of Christ proclaims that Jesus will return gloriously at the end of time to judge the living and the dead. This parousia (coming) will be visible and universal, marking the end of history and the beginning of the definitive kingdom of God.

Biblical basis: Matthew 24:30, Acts 1:11, 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17, Revelation 1:7

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, part of the Creed

Historical context: The second coming of Christ was always part of Christian faith from apostolic times, being mentioned in the Apostles' Creed and always defended by the Church as the hope and certainty of the faithful.

Resurrection of the Dead

The dogma of the resurrection of the dead proclaims that at the end of time, all the dead will rise with their bodies, which will be reunited with their souls. The just will rise with glorious bodies, while the condemned will rise for eternal damnation.

Biblical basis: John 5:28-29, 1 Corinthians 15:12-57, Revelation 20:12-13

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, part of the Creed

Historical context: The resurrection of the dead was always part of Christian faith, being mentioned in the Creed and always defended against philosophies that denied bodily resurrection, establishing that salvation involves both soul and body.
