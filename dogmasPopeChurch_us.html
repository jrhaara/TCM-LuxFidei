Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

The dogmas about the Pope and the Church reveal the nature, authority, and mission of the Catholic Church. These dogmas include papal infallibility, the authority of the magisterium, and the nature of the Church as the Mystical Body of Christ.

Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

23. The Church was founded by the God-Man, Jesus Christ

Explanation: Christ founded the Church; He established the substantial foundations of it, regarding its doctrine, worship, and constitution. The Church is not a human creation, but was instituted directly by Jesus Christ during his earthly life.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: The Church has always defended that it was founded by Christ, establishing that it is the visible continuation of Christ's mission on earth, with the Apostles as its first foundations.

Official or biblical citation: "Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by flesh and blood, but by my Father in heaven. And I tell you that you are Peter (in Aramaic: Kepha = Rock), and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it." (Matthew 16:17-19)

Spiritual application: Recognize the Church as a divine work of Christ, love it and serve it as the mystical body of Christ on earth.

Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

24. Christ constituted the Apostle Saint Peter as first among the Apostles and as visible head of the whole Church, conferring on him immediately and personally the primacy of jurisdiction

Explanation: The Roman Pontiff is the successor of the blessed Saint Peter and has the earthly primacy over the whole flock of the Lord, which is the Church. Christ personally chose Peter to be the visible foundation of the Church and conferred on him special authority over the other Apostles.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially against schisms

Historical context: The Church has always defended the primacy of Peter and his successors (the Popes), establishing that the Pope is the visible foundation of the unity of the Church and possesses supreme authority over the whole Church.

Official or biblical citation: "Simon son of John, do you love me more than these? (...) Feed my lambs. (...) Take care of my sheep." (John 21:15-17) / "I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers." (Luke 22:31-32)

Spiritual application: Recognize and respect the authority of the Pope as successor of Peter, uniting with him in the communion of the Church and following his teachings.

Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

25. The Pope possesses full and supreme power of jurisdiction over the whole Church, not only in matters of faith and morals, but also in the discipline and government of the Church

Explanation: According to this declaration, the power of the Pope is of jurisdiction; universal; supreme; full; ordinary; episcopal; immediate. The Pope possesses complete authority over the whole Church, not only in matters of faith and morals, but also in matters of discipline and government.

When it was proclaimed: First Vatican Council (1870)

Developed by: Medieval and modern theologians, especially against Gallicanism and Conciliarism

Historical context: In response to movements that questioned the authority of the Pope (Gallicanism, Conciliarism), the First Vatican Council defined that the Pope possesses supreme and full power over the whole Church.

Official or biblical citation: "I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven; whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven." (Matthew 16:19)

Spiritual application: Obey the Pope as the supreme authority of the Church, recognizing that his authority comes from Christ and serves the good of the whole Church.

Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

26. The Pope is infallible when he speaks ex cathedra

Explanation: To understand this dogma, it is good to remember: the subject of papal infallibility is every legitimate Pope, in his capacity as successor of Peter. The object of infallibility is the truths of faith and morals, revealed or in intimate connection with Divine Revelation. The condition of infallibility is that the Pope speak ex cathedra, that is: that he speak as pastor and teacher of all the faithful making use of his supreme authority, and that he have the intention of defining some doctrine of faith or morals to be believed by all the faithful. The reason for infallibility is the supernatural assistance of the Holy Spirit, which preserves the supreme teacher of the Church from all error.

When it was proclaimed: First Vatican Council (1870)

Developed by: Medieval and modern theologians, especially against Gallicanism

Historical context: In response to movements that questioned the authority of the Pope, the First Vatican Council defined that the Pope possesses infallibility when he speaks officially on faith and morals, establishing the limits and conditions of this prerogative.

Official or biblical citation: "And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age." (Matthew 28:20) / "I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers." (Luke 22:32)

Spiritual application: Trust in the ex cathedra definitions of the Pope, recognizing that they are assisted by the Holy Spirit and are irreformable, serving as a sure guide for faith.

Dogmas about the Pope and the Church

27. The Church is infallible when it makes definitions in matters of faith and morals

Explanation: Subject to infallibility are: The Pope, when he speaks ex cathedra; The full episcopate, with the Pope, who is the head of the episcopate, is infallible when, gathered in an ecumenical council or dispersed throughout the flock of the Earth, it teaches and promotes a truth of faith or morals to be held by all the faithful. The Church, as a teaching body, is preserved from error when it teaches officially on faith and morals.

When it was proclaimed: First Vatican Council (1870)

Developed by: Church Fathers and theologians, especially against heresies

Historical context: The infallibility of the Church has always been recognized since the first councils. The First Vatican Council clearly defined that both the Pope and the episcopate in communion with him are infallible when they teach officially on faith and morals.

Official or biblical citation: "And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age." (Matthew 28:20) / "The church of the living God, the pillar and foundation of the truth." (1 Timothy 3:15)

Spiritual application: Trust in the magisterium of the Church, recognizing that it is assisted by the Holy Spirit to preserve and transmit revealed truth without error.
