Dogmas about the Sacraments

The dogmas about the sacraments reveal that they are effective signs of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted to the Church. These dogmas include the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist, the efficacy of the sacraments, and the seven sacraments of the Church.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

28. Baptism is a true Sacrament instituted by Jesus Christ

Explanation: Baptism is necessary for salvation, erases original sin and all personal sins, confers sanctifying grace and incorporates the baptized into the Church. Baptism imprints an indelible character on the soul, being valid only once.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1547), against Anabaptism

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: In response to movements that denied the necessity of baptism or that defended rebaptism, the Church defined that baptism is necessary for salvation and confers grace ex opere operato.

Official or biblical citation: "Jesus said to them, 'All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.'" (Matthew 28:18-19) / "No one can enter the kingdom of God unless they are born of water and the Spirit." (John 3:5)

Spiritual application: Value baptism as the gateway to Christian life, renew baptismal promises and live as children of God reborn by water and the Spirit.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

29. Confirmation is a true and proper Sacrament

Explanation: This Sacrament grants to the baptized the Strength of the Holy Spirit so that they may be strengthened interiorly in their supernatural life and confess exteriorly with courage their faith in Jesus Christ. Confirmation imprints an indelible character and completes baptismal grace.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1547)

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: Confirmation was always recognized as a sacrament distinct from baptism, being defined by the Council of Trent as one of the seven sacraments instituted by Christ.

Official or biblical citation: "When the apostles in Jerusalem heard that Samaria had accepted the word of God, they sent Peter and John to them. When they arrived, they prayed for the new believers there that they might receive the Holy Spirit." (Acts 8:14-15)

Spiritual application: Seek confirmation to receive the fullness of the Holy Spirit and be strengthened to witness the faith with courage and live as a mature Christian.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

30. The Church received from Christ the power to forgive sins committed after Baptism

Explanation: The power to forgive and to retain sins to reconcile the faithful who have fallen after Baptism was communicated to the Apostles and to their legitimate successors. Christ, who can forgive sins, gave to his Church the power to forgive them in his Name.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: The Church has always recognized that it received from Christ the power to forgive sins, establishing that priests, acting in the name of Christ, can absolve the sins of penitents.

Official or biblical citation: "Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive anyone's sins, their sins are forgiven; if you do not forgive them, they are not forgiven." (John 20:22-23)

Spiritual application: Make frequent use of the sacrament of reconciliation, recognizing the power of Christ to forgive through his ministers and seeking continuous conversion.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

31. Sacramental Confession of sins is prescribed by Divine Law and is necessary for salvation

Explanation: It is enough to indicate the guilt of conscience to a properly ordained priest, through secret confession. Individual confession is necessary for grave sins. The priest, acting in the name of Christ, absolves the sins of the penitent who repents, confesses, and has purpose of amendment.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1551), against Protestantism

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially against theories that denied the necessity of confession

Historical context: In response to theories that denied the necessity of confession or the power to forgive sins, the Council of Trent defined that confession is necessary and that priests have the power to absolve sins.

Official or biblical citation: "Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed." (James 5:16) / "If you forgive anyone's sins, their sins are forgiven." (John 20:23)

Spiritual application: Practice confession regularly, especially for grave sins, recognizing that it is a necessary means of reconciliation with God and with the Church.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

32. The Eucharist is a true Sacrament instituted by Christ

Explanation: The Eucharist is the central sacrament of the Church, instituted by Christ at the Last Supper. In the Eucharist, the bread and wine are truly transformed into the Body and Blood of Christ. Christ is really present, substantially, under the appearances of bread and wine.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1551), against Protestantism

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially against the Berengarians

Historical context: In response to theories that denied the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist, the Council of Trent defined dogmatically transubstantiation, establishing that Christ is truly present in the sacrament.

Official or biblical citation: "I am the living bread that came down from heaven. Whoever eats this bread will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world." (John 6:51) / "My flesh is real food and my blood is real drink." (John 6:55)

Spiritual application: Participate frequently in the Eucharist, recognizing the real presence of Christ and receiving Him with devotion and reverence, as spiritual food for eternal life.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

33. Christ is present in the Sacrament of the Altar by Transubstantiation of the whole substance of bread into his Body and the whole substance of wine into his Blood

Explanation: Transubstantiation is a conversion in the passive sense; it is the passage from one thing to another. The substances of Bread and Wine cease, for in their places succeed the Body and Blood of Christ. Transubstantiation is a miraculous and singular conversion different from natural conversions, because not only the matter but also the form of bread and wine are converted; only the accidents remain unchanged: we continue to see and experience physically bread and wine, but substantially they are no longer so, because in them is really the Body, the Blood, the Soul and the Divinity of Our Lord Jesus Christ.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1551)

Developed by: Medieval theologians, especially Saint Thomas Aquinas

Historical context: The Council of Trent defined the doctrine of transubstantiation to explain how bread and wine are truly transformed into the Body and Blood of Christ, maintaining only the sensible appearances.

Official or biblical citation: "Is not the cup of thanksgiving for which we give thanks a participation in the blood of Christ? And is not the bread that we break a participation in the body of Christ?" (1 Corinthians 10:16) / "Everyone ought to examine themselves before they eat of the bread and drink from the cup. For those who eat and drink without discerning the body of Christ eat and drink judgment on themselves." (1 Corinthians 11:28-30)

Spiritual application: Adore Christ present in the Eucharist, recognizing that under the appearances of bread and wine the Lord is really present, and receive communion with faith and reverence.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

34. Anointing of the Sick is a true and proper Sacrament instituted by Christ

Explanation: This sacrament confers special grace on the sick and elderly in danger of death. The anointing with consecrated oil strengthens the soul, forgives venial sins and can restore physical health if it is God's will. It is a sacrament of healing and strengthening for those who suffer.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1551), Second Vatican Council (1962-1965)

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: Anointing of the Sick was always practiced by the Church. The Council of Trent defined it as a sacrament, and the Second Vatican Council reformulated its practice, extending it not only to the dying, but to all seriously ill.

Official or biblical citation: "Is anyone among you sick? Let them call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them with oil in the name of the Lord." (James 5:14)

Spiritual application: Seek the anointing of the sick when ill or in danger, trusting in the power of Christ to heal and strengthen, and offer sufferings in union with the sufferings of Christ.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

35. Holy Orders is a true and proper Sacrament instituted by Christ

Explanation: There exists a hierarchy instituted by Divine ordination, which consists of Bishops, Presbyters and Deacons. This sacrament confers the sacred power to serve the Church as bishop, presbyter or deacon. Holy Orders imprints an indelible character on the soul.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1547)

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: In response to movements that denied the sacramental character of Holy Orders, the Council of Trent defined that Holy Orders is a sacrament instituted by Christ, establishing its characteristics and effects.

Official or biblical citation: "Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, To all God's holy people in Christ Jesus at Philippi, together with the overseers and deacons." (Philippians 1:1) / "Do not neglect your gift, which was given you through prophecy when the body of elders laid their hands on you." (1 Timothy 4:14)

Spiritual application: Respect and support ordained ministers, recognizing that they have received a special gift from Christ to serve the Church, and pray for priestly vocations.

Dogmas about the Sacraments

36. Matrimony is a true and proper Sacrament

Explanation: Christ restored Matrimony instituted and blessed by God, making it recover its primitive ideal of unity and indissolubility and elevating it to the dignity of a Sacrament. Matrimony is the sacrament that unites a man and a woman in an indissoluble union, symbol of the union of Christ with the Church.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Trent (1563)

Developed by: Church Fathers from the first centuries

Historical context: In response to movements that denied the sacramental character of matrimony, the Council of Trent defined that matrimony is a sacrament instituted by Christ, establishing its characteristics of unity and indissolubility.

Official or biblical citation: "For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh." (Genesis 2:24) / "Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate." (Matthew 19:6) / "This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church." (Ephesians 5:32)

Spiritual application: Live matrimony as a sacrament, seeking mutual sanctification, fidelity and indissolubility, and recognizing that marriage is a path of holiness and image of the union of Christ with the Church.
