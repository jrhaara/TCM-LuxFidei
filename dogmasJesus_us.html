Dogmas about Jesus Christ

Dogmas about Jesus Christ reveal the truth about the person and work of the Savior. These dogmas affirm the divinity and humanity of Christ, his redemptive mission, and his real presence in the Eucharist, forming the heart of Christian faith.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

6. Jesus Christ is true God and Son of God by Essence

Explanation: Christ possesses the infinite Divine Nature with all its infinite Perfections, for having been eternally generated by God. Jesus Christ is true God, consubstantial with the Father, not merely a prophet or a special man, but the very Son of God, the second Person of the Holy Trinity.

When it was proclaimed: First Council of Nicaea (325 AD)

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially Saint Athanasius

Historical context: In response to the Arian heresy that denied the divinity of Christ, the Council of Nicaea defined that Jesus is "consubstantial with the Father" (homoousios), establishing that He is true God from all eternity.

Official or biblical citation: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." (John 1:1) / "I and the Father are one." (John 10:30)

Spiritual application: Worship Jesus Christ as true God, recognizing his divinity and trusting in his saving power.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

7. Jesus possesses two natures that do not transform, mix, or confuse

Explanation: The Council of Chalcedon (451, IV) declares: "Our Lord Jesus Christ, Himself perfect in Divinity and Himself perfect in humanity (...) which must be recognized in two natures: without confusion, without change, without division, without separation and in no way is the difference of nature abolished because of the union, each nature preserving its property and concurring in one Person" (Dz. 148). Christ is the possessor of an integral Divine Nature and an integral human nature.

When it was proclaimed: Council of Chalcedon (451 AD)

Developed by: Saint Leo the Great, Fathers of the Council of Chalcedon

Historical context: In response to heresies that denied the true humanity of Christ (Docetism) or affirmed only one nature (Monophysitism), the Church defined that Christ possesses two complete natures: divine and human, united without confusion.

Official or biblical citation: "The Word became flesh and dwelt among us." (John 1:14) / "Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be used to his own advantage; rather, he made himself nothing by taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness." (Philippians 2:6-7)

Spiritual application: Recognize in Christ the perfect union of the divine and the human, model of our own redeemed humanity.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

8. Each nature in Christ possesses its own physical will and physical operation

Explanation: The Third Council of Constantinople (680-681) declares: "We proclaim, according to the teachings of the Holy Fathers, that there are two physical wills and two physical operations, in an indivisible way, in a non-convertible way, in an inseparable way and in a non-confused way. And these two physical wills do not oppose each other." Despite the physical duality of the two wills, there existed and exists moral unity, because the human will of Christ conforms in free subordination, in a most perfect way, to the Divine Will.

When it was proclaimed: Third Council of Constantinople (680-681 AD)

Developed by: Fathers of the Third Council of Constantinople, against Monothelitism

Historical context: In response to the Monothelite heresy that affirmed that Christ had only one will (divine), the Council defined that Christ possesses two wills: one divine and one human, which are perfectly harmonized.

Official or biblical citation: "Not as I will, but as you will." (Matthew 26:39) / "I have come down from heaven not to do my will but to do the will of him who sent me." (John 6:38)

Spiritual application: Imitate Christ by conforming our human will to the divine will, following his example of perfect obedience to the Father.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

9. Jesus Christ, though man, is the Natural Son of God the Father

Explanation: Jesus Christ, even though possessing complete human nature, is the Natural Son of God the Father by nature, not by adoption. His divine sonship is eternal and essential, not acquired. He is the Son of God from all eternity, even before the incarnation.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially against Arianism and Adoptionism

Historical context: The Church has always defended that Christ is the Son of God by nature, not by adoption, establishing that his divine sonship is eternal and essential to his person.

Official or biblical citation: "But when the set time had fully come, God sent his Son, born of a woman, born under the law, to redeem those under the law, that we might receive adoption to sonship." (Galatians 4:4-5)

Spiritual application: Recognize Christ as the only Son of God and, through him, receive the grace to become adopted children of the Father.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

10. Christ immolated Himself on the Cross as a true and proper Sacrifice

Explanation: In the ineffable Mystery, Christ, by his human nature, was at the same time Priest and Offering, but by his Divine Nature, together with the Father and the Holy Spirit, was the Same One who received the Sacrifice. The death of Christ on the cross was a true sacrifice, offered voluntarily for the salvation of humanity.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially Saint Augustine and Saint Thomas Aquinas

Historical context: The Church has always taught that the death of Christ was a true sacrifice, establishing that Christ is at the same time priest, victim, and altar of the redemptive sacrifice.

Official or biblical citation: "Christ loved us and gave himself up for us as a fragrant offering and sacrifice to God." (Ephesians 5:2) / "Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29)

Spiritual application: Participate in the sacrifice of Christ through the Eucharist, offering our life as a spiritual sacrifice pleasing to God.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

11. Christ redeemed and reconciled us with God through the Sacrifice of his death on the Cross

Explanation: Jesus Christ willed to offer Himself to God the Father, as a Sacrifice presented on the altar of the Cross in his Death, to obtain for us eternal forgiveness. Through his passion, death, and resurrection, Christ redeemed humanity from sin and eternal death, reconciling man with God.

When it was proclaimed: Information not officially recorded.

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially Saint Augustine and Saint Thomas Aquinas

Historical context: The doctrine of redemption was developed over the centuries, explaining how the death of Christ, being an act of infinite love, can save all humanity and satisfy divine justice.

Official or biblical citation: "For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God." (1 Peter 3:18) / "God was reconciling the world to himself in Christ." (2 Corinthians 5:19)

Spiritual application: Live in gratitude for the redemption obtained by Christ, seeking reconciliation with God and with our brothers through repentance and conversion.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

12. On the third day after his Death, Christ rose glorious from the dead

Explanation: On the third day, risen by his own Virtue, Our Lord Jesus rose from the tomb. His resurrection was a historical and real event, not a mere spiritual appearance, confirming his divinity and guaranteeing the future resurrection of all the faithful.

When it was proclaimed: From the earliest times of the Church, confirmed by the Councils

Developed by: Apostles and Church Fathers from the beginning

Historical context: The resurrection of Christ is the foundation of Christian faith from the beginning. The Apostles witnessed this event and the Church has always defended it as a fundamental historical and theological truth.

Official or biblical citation: "He is not here; he has risen, just as he said. Come and see the place where he lay." (Matthew 28:6) / "If Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless and so is your faith." (1 Corinthians 15:14)

Spiritual application: Live in the hope of the resurrection, trusting that, as Christ rose, we too will rise to eternal life.

Dogmas about Jesus Christ

13. Christ ascended in Body and Soul to Heaven and is seated at the right hand of God the Father

Explanation: He rose from the dead and ascended to Heaven in Body and Soul. Christ, forty days after his resurrection, ascended into heaven in body and soul, sitting at the right hand of the Father. This ascension marks the glorification of Christ and his entry into heavenly glory, from where he intercedes for us.

When it was proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, confirmed by the Apostles' Creed

Developed by: Apostles and Church Fathers from the beginning

Historical context: The ascension of Christ was witnessed by the Apostles and has always been part of the Christian Creed, symbolizing the definitive entry of Christ into glory and his permanent presence with the Father.

Official or biblical citation: "He was taken up before their very eyes, and a cloud hid him from their sight." (Acts 1:9) / "After the Lord Jesus had spoken to them, he was taken up into heaven and he sat at the right hand of God." (Mark 16:19)

Spiritual application: Live with the certainty that Christ is in heaven interceding for us, and hope for the day when we too will be raised to heavenly glory.
