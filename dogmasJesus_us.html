Dogmas about Jesus Christ

Dogmas about Jesus Christ reveal the truth about the person and work of the Savior. These dogmas affirm the divinity and humanity of Christ, his redemptive mission, and his real presence in the Eucharist, forming the heart of Christian faith.

The Divinity of Christ

The dogma of the divinity of Christ proclaims that Jesus Christ is true God, consubstantial with the Father. He is not merely a prophet or a special man, but the very Son of God, the second Person of the Holy Trinity, who became incarnate to save humanity.

Proclaimed: First Council of Nicaea (325 AD)

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially Saint Athanasius

Historical context: In response to the Arian heresy that denied the divinity of Christ, the Council of Nicaea defined that Jesus is "consubstantial with the Father" (homoousios), establishing that He is true God from all eternity.

The Humanity of Christ

The dogma of the humanity of Christ affirms that Jesus Christ is true man, possessing a complete human nature, body and soul, except for sin. He experienced all human limitations, except error and sin, being true God and true man.

Proclaimed: First Council of Constantinople (381 AD) and Council of Chalcedon (451 AD)

Developed by: Church Fathers against Docetist and Monophysite heresies

Historical context: In response to heresies that denied the true humanity of Christ (Docetism) or affirmed only one nature (Monophysitism), the Church defined that Christ possesses two complete natures: divine and human.

The Hypostatic Union

The dogma of the hypostatic union proclaims that in Jesus Christ the two natures, divine and human, are united in one Person (hypostasis), the Person of the Word. The natures do not mix or confuse, but remain distinct in a perfect union.

Proclaimed: Council of Chalcedon (451 AD)

Developed by: Saint Leo the Great, Fathers of the Council of Chalcedon

Historical context: The Council of Chalcedon defined that Christ is "one Person in two natures," establishing the classic formula that remains to this day: "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."

Redemption

The dogma of redemption affirms that Jesus Christ, through his passion, death, and resurrection, redeemed humanity from sin and eternal death. His death on the cross was a voluntary and effective sacrifice that reconciled man with God and opened the gates of heaven.

Biblical basis: Gospels, Letters of Saint Paul

Developed by: Church Fathers, especially Saint Augustine and Saint Thomas Aquinas

Historical context: The doctrine of redemption was developed over the centuries, explaining how the death of Christ, being an act of infinite love, can save all humanity and satisfy divine justice.

The Resurrection

The dogma of the resurrection proclaims that Jesus Christ rose from the dead on the third day after his crucifixion. His resurrection was a historical and real event, not a mere spiritual appearance, confirming his divinity and guaranteeing the future resurrection of all the faithful.

Biblical basis: Gospels, especially Matthew 28, Mark 16, Luke 24, John 20-21

Proclaimed: From the earliest times of the Church, confirmed by the Councils

Historical context: The resurrection of Christ is the foundation of Christian faith from the beginning. The Apostles witnessed this event and the Church has always defended it as a fundamental historical and theological truth.

The Ascension

The dogma of the ascension affirms that Jesus Christ, forty days after his resurrection, ascended into heaven in body and soul, sitting at the right hand of the Father. This ascension marks the glorification of Christ and his entry into heavenly glory, from where he intercedes for us.

Biblical basis: Acts of the Apostles 1:9-11, Luke 24:50-53

Proclaimed: From the earliest centuries, confirmed by the Apostles' Creed

Historical context: The ascension of Christ was witnessed by the Apostles and has always been part of the Christian Creed, symbolizing the definitive entry of Christ into glory and his permanent presence with the Father.
